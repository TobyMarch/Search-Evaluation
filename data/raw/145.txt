<p>
“We’ve
been
<a
href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/music/the-beatles/"><strong>Beatles</strong></a>
for
a
long
time,”
John
Lennon
tells

at
the

Prince
of
Wales

“Ever

was
a
small
boy
I’ve
been
studying

this

</p>









</div>









<div
class="secondPar">











<p>
John,
short
and
tanned,
with
dark
hair
and
a
hint
of
eyeliner,
does
not
look
much
like
Lennon,
because

isn't.
The
John
Lennon
reclining
in
a
chair
is
Michael
Gagliano,
breaking
from
a
rehearsal
for
new
Beatles
musical
<i>Let
It
Be</i>.
</p>









</div>









<div
class="thirdPar">











<p>
From
tonight,
Gagliano
must
become
a
living
Lennon
for
two
and
a
half
hours
as
part
of
the
cast
of
the
musical,
which
follows
the
Beatles’
music
from
‘Love
Me
Do’
in
1962
to
their
breakup
in
1970.
</p>









</div>









<div
class="fourthPar">











<p>
Those
involved
in
Let
It
Be
aren’t
attempting
to
rewrite
the
story
of
the
Beatles,
and
have
opted
instead
for
a
hyper-precise
tribute,
all
songs
and
and
no
dialogue,
that
they
hope
will
emulate
the
look
and
sound
of
their
heroes
well
enough
for
the
audience
to
believe
they
are
watching
the
Liverpool
lads
with
the
sixties
in
full
swing.
</p>









</div>









<div
class="fifthPar">











<p>
It
is
a
gamble.
Almost
none
of
the
cast
look
like
their
corresponding
Beatles,
and
they
range
from
a
Ringo
fresh
out
of
music
college
to
Pauls
pushing
40.
There
are
two
men
per
Beatle,
eight
in
all.
</p>









</div>
