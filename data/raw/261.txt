<h1
class="title">Top
10
Bee
Gees
Songs
in
memory
of
Robin
Gibb
</h1>








<div
id="content-area">









<div
id="node-279393"
class="node
node-type-feature
clearfix">










<div
class="meta">











<span
class="author-name"><a
href="/columnists/Daniel+S+Levine">Daniel
S
Levine</a></span>










<br
/>











<span
class="submitted">5/21/2012</span>










</div>










<div
class="content">











<p>It’s
awful
to
hear
the
news
that
Robin
Gibb,
one
of
the
founding
members
of
the
Bee
Gees,
has
died.
Robin’s
vocals
were
a
key
part
of
the
group,
which
produced
some
of
the
most
beautiful
harmonies
ever
committed
to
record.
The
group’s
influence
on
the
world
of
music
was
immense.
We
all
know
their
disco
work,
but
they
had
been
around
for
over
a
decade
before
“Jive
Talkin’”
ever
hit
the
airwaves.
</p>











<p>Here’s
a
list
of
their
major
songs
and
a
few
personal
favorites.
As
with
any
list,
if
you
ask
me
tomorrow,
this
will
probably
be
very
different.
You
can
let
us
know
your
favorite
songs
in
the
comments
section.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/hW2tGPvsR2E"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
10.
“This
Is
Where
I
Came
In”
-
<i>This
Is
Where
I
Came
In</i>,
2001<br
/>
<i>This
Is
Where
I
Came
In</i>
was
the
group’s
last
album,
recorded
shortly
before
Maurice,
Robin’s
twin
brother,
died
in
2003.
The
title
track
was
also
released
as
a
single
and
features
a
fantastic
groove
that
harkens
back
to
their
great
mid-’60s
records.
Robin’s
vocals
here
prove
that
he
never
lost
his
power
to
give
an
emotional
performance.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/xgDbE6WOyws"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
9.
“Fanny
(Be
Tender
With
My
Love)”
-
<i>Main
Course</i>,
1975<br
/>
It’s
really
insane
to
think
that
a
group
of
English-born,
Australian-raised
musicians
charged
the
course
of
music
history
by
making
disco
mainstream.
Their
three
disco
records,
plus
the
songs
on
<i>Saturday
Night
Fever</i>,
is
one
of
the
most
stunning
streaks
of
success
in
music
history.
“Fanny
(Be
Tender
With
My
Love)”
is
just
one
of
the
three
hits
on
<i>Main
Course</i>,
but
is
a
favorite
of
mine.
It’s
a
wonderful
track,
with
that
perfect
blend
of
voices
that
was
so
unique
to
the
group.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/L7kbQyseI1I"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
8.
“Spicks
and
Specks”
-
<i>Spicks
and
Specks</i>,
1966<br
/>
By
the
time
they
released
<i>Bee
Gees’
1st</i>
worldwide,
the
group
already
recorded
two
albums
in
Australia.
“Spicks
and
Specks”
was
the
single
that
gained
international
notice.
It’s
primarily
a
Barry-driven
track,
with
the
oldest
brother
taking
lead
vocals
and
is
very
primitive
compared
to
their
later
work,
but
it’s
still
a
great
slice
of
pop.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/AbkbGF27JyY"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
7.
“Massachusetts”
-
<i>Horizontal</i>,
1967<br
/>
“Massachusetts”
was
the
group’s
first
UK
No.
1
single
and
is
still
one
of
the
best-selling
singles
in
the
UK
of
all
time.
If
this
is
the
track
that
acted
as
an
introduction
to
the
group
for
some,
they
couldn’t
have
asked
for
a
better
intro.
Robin’s
vocal
is
perfect.
I
love
it
when
they
sing
“And
the
lights,
all
went
down
in
Massachusetts/The
day
I
left
her
standing
on
her
own.”
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/nREV8bQJ1MA"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
6.
“Too
Much
Heaven”
-
<i>Spirits
Having
Flown</i>,
1979<br
/>
<i>Spirits
Having
Flown</i>
is
such
a
fantastic
record
from
top
to
bottom
-
especially
from
the
top.
The
album’s
first
three
tracks
all
reached
No.
1
on
Billboard.
“Too
Much
Heaven”
was
written
for
UNICEF
and
was
released
a
few
months
before
the
rest
of
the
album.
Still,
it
features
the
group’s
massive
overdubbing
addiction
(is
that
a
thing?),
which
makes
the
three
members
of
the
group
sound
like
a
choir.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/h-6kKCX2q1I"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
5.
“Melody
Fair”
-
<i>Odessa</i>,
1969<br
/>
<i>Odessa</i>
is
the
Bee
Gees’
<i>White
Album</i>,
a
double
LP
filled
with
tracks
that
cover
so
many
genres
it
makes
the
mind
boggle.
It
wasn’t
as
successful
as
the
Beatles’
seminal
album,
but
it
features
some
fantastic
tracks,
including
“Melody
Fair.”
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/XBw25CrUS-o"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
4.
“Jive
Talkin’”
-
<i>Main
Course</i>,
1975<br
/>
To
me,
“Jive
Talkin’”
is
the
Bee
Gees
telling
the
world,
“Hey,
we’re
still
here
and
we
still
write
songs
better
than
anyone
else!”
The
track,
which
kicks
off
with
that
deep
beat,
was
released
after
years
of
flops
and
suddenly
made
the
group
relevant
again.
It
proved
so
popular
that
it
was
included
on
the
<i>Saturday
Night
Fever</i>
soundtrack...even
though
it
is
never
heard
in
the
film!
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/_JoZS6LgqYI"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
3.
“You
Should
Be
Dancing”
-
<i>Children
of
the
World</i>,
1976<br
/>
“You
Should
Be
Dancing”
<i>was</i>
in
<i>Saturday
Night
Fever</i>
after
it
was
issued
as
a
single
and
acted
as
the
lead-off
track
to
<i>Children
of
the
World</i>.
It
features
stunning
vocals
and
amazing
production.
It’s
hard
not
to
want
to
get
up
and
dance
every
time
you
hear
it.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/I_izvAbhExY"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
2.
“Stayin’
Alive”
-
<i>Saturday
Night
Fever</i>,
1977<br
/>
It’s
not
my
favorite
Bee
Gees
song,
but
people
love
it
and
it
continues
to
get
airtime
decades
since
disco
fell
out
of
popularity.
The
video
of
the
group
walking
to
the
song
remains
immortal.
(The
video
I’ve
included
here
has
been
viewed
over
14
million
times
since
the
group’s
label
posted
it
in
2009.)
It
seems
to
encapsulate
an
entire
genre
in
one
song.
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/pTQiT58AbE0"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
1.
“How
Can
You
Mend
A
Broken
Heart”
-
<i>Trafalgar</i>,
1971<br
/>
Four
minutes
of
perfection.
It
just
doesn’t
get
any
better
than
this.
My
favorite
song
by
the
group
is
either
this
or
“To
Love
Somebody,”
but
I’ll
pick
this
one
for
Robin’s
amazing
vocal
on
the
first
verse.
The
track
slowly
builds
-
it
feels
more
like
a
march
than
a
traditional
song
-
with
all
three
brothers
coming
together
for
the
final
verse.
</p>











<p>Honorable
Mention:
</p>











<p><iframe
width="420"
height="315"
src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/rHQ8oiv0nbw"
frameborder="0"
allowfullscreen=""></iframe><br
/>
“Saved
By
The
Bell”
-
<i>Robin’s
Reign</i>,
1970<br
/>
Robin
actually
left
the
group
in
1969,
leaving
Barry
and
Maurice
to
record
an
album
by
themselves.
This
track
was
the
single
from
Robin’s
album.
</p>
