class="post-info">Posted
on
Friday,
September
28th,
2012
by
<a
href="http://www.slashfilm.com/author/peter-sciretta/"

<p><a
href="http://bitcast-a-sm.bitgravity.com/slashfilm/wp/wp-content/images/ZZ03E92B23top.jpg"><img
class="aligncenter

wp-image-140859"
title="overcrowded
movie
poster
trend"

/></a></p>










<p>The
latest
All-Dwarf
poster
for
<strong><em>The
Hobbit:
An
Unexpected
Journey</em></strong>
seems
to
confirm
a
new
Hollywood
movie
poster
design
trend
—
filling
a
one-sheet
with
an
overcrowded
gathering
of
characters.
From
what
I
can
tell,
the
new
trend
started
with
the
final
<strong><em>Toy
Story
3</em></strong>
poster,
which
was
created
by
<a
href="http://bltcommunications.com/Home"
target="_blank">BLT
Communications</a>
—
a
marketing
department
Disney
regularly
employs.
The
design
was
pretty
great,
and
almost
everyone
who
wrote
about
it
online
loved
it.
So
its
no
surprise
that
the
design
was
copied
by
a
few
international
marketing
agencies
over
the
past
year.
The
design
concept
was
reused
by
BLT
for
<strong><em>The
Muppets</em></strong>
campaign.
And
this
week
Warner
Bros
has
released
the
all-dwarf
Hobbit
poster
created
by
marketing
company
Ignition
Print.
See
them
all
compared
after
the
jump.</p>

the
Best
&amp;
Worst
Pixar
Movies</a></h1>












<p
class="post-info">Posted
on
Tuesday,
June
26th,
2012
by
<a
href="http://www.slashfilm.com/author/adam-quigley/"
title="Posts
by
Adam
Quigley"
rel="author">Adam
Quigley</a></p>











</div>











<div
class="top-bot"></div>










</div>










<p><a
href="http://bitcast-a-sm.bitgravity.com/slashfilm/wp/wp-content/images/pixar-header.jpg"><img
class="aligncenter
size-medium
wp-image-130157"
title="pixar-header"
src="http://bitcast-a-sm.bitgravity.com/slashfilm/wp/wp-content/images/pixar-header-550x343.jpg"
alt=""
width="550"
height="343"
/></a></p>










<p>How
is
it
that
a
movie
studio
that
produces
kid’s
films
can
be
responsible
for
so
many
of
the
best
films
in
cinema?</p>










<p>Twenty
years
ago,
that
question
would
be
directed
at
Disney.
Now
it’s
more
likely
to
refer
to
Pixar,
Studio
Ghibli,
or
even
Dreamworks
of
late.
What
is
it
about
children’s
entertainment
that
has,
time
and
time
again,
managed
to
capture
the
hearts
and
minds
of
adults
as
much
as
it
has
their
offspring?</p>










<p>Perhaps
it’s
a
result
of
these
films
rekindling
our
lost
sense
of
childlike
wonder
and
naively
adventurous
spirit.
Perhaps
it’s
their
universally
accessible
narrative
simplicity,
always
ready
to
charm
away
our
worries
with
the
awe-inspiring
visual
splendor
through
which
these
tales
are
so
often
told.</p>










<p>Whatever
the
case
may
be,
with
thirteen
films
under
their
belt,
the
Pixar
formula
is
one
that’s
proven
itself
to
leave
a
lasting
impression,
transporting
us
to
spectacular,
gorgeously
rendered
and
thoughtfully
defined
worlds
—
second
only
to
the
passionately
heartfelt
and
funny
stories
of
family
and
friendship
embedded
within.</p>










<p>What’s
more,
Pixar
is
able
to
achieve
this
mixture
while
emboldening
children
to
think
for
themselves;
to
challenge
the
status
quo;
to
recognize
their
true
potential,
as
well
as
their
limitations.
As
fun
and
charming
and
pretty
as
Pixar’s
films
are,
it’s
the
complex
ideas
and
emotions
they
explore
that
makes
them
truly
special,
affording
youths
the
opportunity
to
confront
the
realities
of
the
world
around
them
in
a
way
they
can
understand
and
cope
with.
While
everyone
else
is
content
to
pander
to
kids,
Pixar
knows
that
the
best
way
to
communicate
with
children
is
to
treat
them
as
equals.</p>










<p>But
equality
is
not
a
trait
shared
by
the
current
roster
of
Pixar
films.
Despite
the
technical
virtuosity
on
full
display
with
every
production,
it
takes
a
lot
more
than
stunning
animation
to
make
a
film
great,
and
that’s
not
a
balance
that
Pixar
always
strikes
—
at
least
not
recently.
At
one
point
it
may
have
seemed
like
the
studio
could
do
no
wrong,
but
that
was
a
short-lived
romantic
notion,
and
hardly
one
that
merits
much
deliberation.
No,
far
more
instructive
would
be
to
scrutinize
their
missteps
in
conjunction
with
their
successes,
and
try
to
determine
what
exactly
it
is
that
makes
any
one
of
their
works
richer
than
the
other.
After
all,
what
better
way
to
understand
what
makes
a
story
great
than
to
study
the
best?
<a
href="http://www.slashfilm.com/pixar-in-review-2012-ranking-the-best-worst/#more-130143"
class="more-link">Read
More
&raquo;</a></p>

“Pixar”

<p
class="post-info">Posted
on
Monday,
June
25th,
2012
by
<a

<div

</div>










<p><img
class="aligncenter

wp-image-118400"

<p>This
weekend
saw
the
release
of
Pixar’s
latest
film,
<em>Brave</em>,
a
movie
that
easily
won
the
weekend,
garnering
an
overall
“A”
CinemaScore
from
appreciative
audiences.
Still,
at
only
<a
title="Brave
on
RottenTomatoes"
href="http://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/brave_2012/">74
percent
on
RottenTomatoes</a>
(Pixar’s
second
worst),
and
a
<a
title="SlashFilm's
Brave
Review"
href="http://www.slashfilm.com/brave-review-pixars-latest-hits-target-struggles/">7
out
of
10</a>
from
Germain
Lussier,
it
is
clear
there
is
a
bit
of
room
for
dissent.</p>










<p>Out
there
in
audience-land,
did
you
notice
something
a
little
“off”
about
<em>Brave</em>?
Perhaps
there
are
lessons
that
can
be
learned,
or
conversations
to
engage
in?</p>










<p>To
provide
some
context,
and
on
the
off
chance
we
have
completely
different
taste,
here
are
my
top
five
Pixar
efforts:</p>










<p>1.
<em>WALL-E</em><br
/>
2.
<em>Up</em><br
/>
3.
<em>Toy
Story</em><br
/>
4.
<em>Finding
Nemo</em><br
/>
5.
<em>Monsters,
Inc.</em></p>










<p>Until
now,
the
only
Pixar
film
I
flat
out
didn’t
enjoy
was
<em>Ratatouille</em>,
though
I
admit
to
only
having
seen
it
once,
and
folks
say
I’d
like
it
much
more
if
I
were
to
re-visit.
Even
<em>Cars
2</em>
had
redeeming
qualities.
I
can
truly
say
I’ve
never
found
a
Pixar
film
entirely
lacking,
and
that
statement
includes
<em>Brave</em>.
There’s
no
question
the
film
had
amazing
visuals,
setting
a
new
standard
for
excellence
within
the
animation
genre.
Unfortunately,
the
story
lacked
a
bit
of
…
what’s
the
word
I’m
looking
for?
Ooomph.
As
such,
I’m
compelled
to
break
down
where
I
feel
the
problems
were,
if
only
to
restore
everyone’s
favorite
animation
house
to
the
glory
they
so
richly
deserve.</p>










<p>One
final
note,
just
to
head
off
the
obligatory
“comparing
<em>Brave</em>
to
the
rest
of
Pixar’s
work
isn’t
entirely
fair”
argument,
we’re
in
complete
agreement
there.
It’s
not
fair,
and
in
many
ways
Pixar’s
own
ambition
and
commitment
to
excellence
have
raised
the
bar
for
<em>all</em>
movies.
So
no,
<em>Brave</em>
isn’t
a
bad
movie
on
merit,
it’s
merely
an
average
one,
which
animation
houses
make
all
the
time
without
compelling
anyone
to
write
a
3,000
word
article
on
the
subject.
But
within
the
greater
context
of
Pixar’s
previous
work,
<em>Brave</em>
does
come
up
short,
and
I
think
we’ve
got
a
bead
on
the
reasons
why.</p>

<strong>SPOILERS</strong>
follow,
