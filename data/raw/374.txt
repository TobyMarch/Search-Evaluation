<p><i><b>Watership
Down</b></i>
is
a
1978
British
<a
href="/wiki/Animation?qsrc=3044"
title="Animation">animated</a>
<a
href="/wiki/Adventure_film?qsrc=3044"
title="Adventure
film">adventure</a>
<a
href="/wiki/Drama_film?qsrc=3044"
title="Drama
film">drama
film</a>
written,
produced
and
directed
by
Martin
Rosen
and
based
on
the
<a
href="/wiki/Watership_Down?qsrc=3044"
title="Watership
Down">book
of
the
same
name</a>
by
<a
href="/wiki/Richard_Adams?qsrc=3044"
title="Richard
Adams">Richard
Adams</a>.
It
was
financed
by
a
consortium
of
British
financial
institutions.
Originally
released
on
19
October
1978,
the
film
was
an
immediate
success
and
it
became
the
sixth
most
popular
film
of
1979
at
the
British
box
office.<sup
id="cite_ref-0"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-0">[1]</a></sup>
It
was
one
of
the
first
animated
feature
films
to
be
presented
in
<a
href="/wiki/Dolby?qsrc=3044"
title="Dolby">Dolby</a>.</p>








<p>It
featured
the
voices
of
<a
href="/wiki/John_Hurt?qsrc=3044"
title="John
Hurt">John
Hurt</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/Richard_Briers?qsrc=3044"
title="Richard
Briers">Richard
Briers</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/Harry_Andrews?qsrc=3044"
title="Harry
Andrews">Harry
Andrews</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/Simon_Cadell?qsrc=3044"
title="Simon
Cadell">Simon
Cadell</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/Nigel_Hawthorne?qsrc=3044"
title="Nigel
Hawthorne">Nigel
Hawthorne</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Roy_Kinnear?qsrc=3044"
title="Roy
Kinnear">Roy
Kinnear</a>,
among
others,
and
was
the
last
film
appearance
of
<a
href="/wiki/Zero_Mostel?qsrc=3044"
title="Zero
Mostel">Zero
Mostel</a>,
as
the
voice
of
Kehaar
the
gull.</p>








<p><a
href="/wiki/Art_Garfunkel?qsrc=3044"
title="Art
Garfunkel">Art
Garfunkel</a>'s
British
No.
1
hit,
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Bright_Eyes_(Art_Garfunkel_song)?qsrc=3044"
title="Bright
Eyes
(Art
Garfunkel
song)">Bright
Eyes</a>&quot;,
which
was
written
by
the
British
singer
and
songwriter
<a
href="/wiki/Mike_Batt?qsrc=3044"
title="Mike
Batt">Mike
Batt</a>,
was
also
included.
The
musical
score
was
by
<a
href="/wiki/Angela_Morley?qsrc=3044"
title="Angela
Morley">Angela
Morley</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Malcolm_Williamson?qsrc=3044"
title="Malcolm
Williamson">Malcolm
Williamson</a>.</p>

<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Plot">Plot</span></h2>








<p>According
to
<a
href="/wiki/Lapine_language?qsrc=3044"
title="Lapine
language">Lapine</a>
culture
and
mythology,
the
world
was
created
by
the
god
Frith,
who
represents
the
Sun.
All
animals
lived
harmoniously,
but
the
rabbits
eventually
multiplied,
and
their
appetite
led
to
a
food
shortage.
At
the
prayers
of
the
desperate
animals,
Frith
warned
the
rabbit
prince
El-ahrairah
to
control
his
people,
but
was
scoffed
at.
In
retaliation,
Frith
gave
special
gifts
to
every
animal,
but
some
animals
he
made
predators
to
prey
upon
the
rabbits.
Satisfied
that
El-ahrairah
had
learned
his
lesson,
Frith
also
gave
the
rabbits
speed
and
cunning;
while
many
would
seek
to
kill
them,
the
rabbits
could
survive
by
their
wits
and
quickness.</p>








<p>In
the
present,
in
the
English
countryside
of
Sandleford,
Fiver,
a
rabbit
<a
href="/wiki/Clairvoyance?qsrc=3044"
title="Clairvoyance">seer</a>
has
an
apocalyptic
vision
and
goes
with
his
older
brother
Hazel
to
beg
the
chief
to
have
the
warren
evacuated,
but
they
are
dismissed
and
attempt
to
make
an
exodus
themselves.
The
group
meets
resistance
from
the
warren's
police
force
called
the
Owsla,
but
eight
manage
to
fight
and
escape,
including
Fiver,
Hazel,
Bigwig,
Blackberry,
Pipkin,
Dandelion,
Silver
and
Violet.
They
travel
through
the
dangerous
woods
and
make
it
to
a
bean
field
to
rest.
In
the
morning,
Violet
is
killed
by
a
hawk,
leaving
the
group
without
a
female.</p>








<p>After
several
dangerous
situations,
they
meet
the
enigmatic
rabbit
Cowslip,
who
invites
them
to
his
warren.
They
are
grateful,
but
Fiver
senses
something
bizarre
in
the
atmosphere,
and
the
resident
rabbits'
overly
resigned
attitudes,
and
leaves.
An
irked
Bigwig
follows,
and
chastises
Fiver
for
supposedly
causing
senseless
tension
with
his
instincts.
Moments
later,
however,
he
is
caught
in
a
<a
href="/wiki/Animal_trapping?qsrc=3044"
title="Animal
trapping">snare
trap</a>.
Fiver
attempts
to
get
help
from
their
hosts,
but
is
ignored.
Bigwig
is
freed
after
nearly
dying.
As
Fiver
reveals,
the
warren
is
fed
by
a
farmer
who
snares
rabbits
in
return
for
his
food
and
protection
from
predators.
After
Bigwig's
narrow
escape,
the
other
rabbits
willingly
follow
Fiver's
and
Hazel's
advice
and
set
out
once
more.</p>








<p>The
rabbits
discover
Nuthanger
farm,
which
contains
a
<a
href="/wiki/Hutch_(animal_cage)?qsrc=3044"
title="Hutch
(animal
cage)">hutch</a>
of
female
rabbits,
necessary
for
a
new
warren.
However,
they
do
not
manage
to
free
them,
on
account
of
the
territorial
farm
animals.
They
find
the
injured
Owsla
captain
Holly,
who
recounts
the
destruction
of
Sandleford
by
humans,
and
a
mysterious
group
called
the
&quot;Efrafrans&quot;
before
falling
unconscious.
Fiver
finally
leads
the
group
to
the
hill
he
envisioned,
<a
href="/wiki/Watership_Down,_Hampshire?qsrc=3044"
title="Watership
Down,
Hampshire">Watership
Down</a>,
where
the
rabbits
settle.</p>








<p>They
settle
in,
developing
their
own
warren,
with
Hazel
as
chief.
They
befriend
an
acerbic
injured
<a
href="/wiki/Seagull?qsrc=3044"
title="Seagull">seagull</a>,
Kehaar,
who
offers
to
survey
the
local
area
for
does.
The
rabbits
return
to
Nuthanger
to
free
the
does;
Hazel
is
shot
by
a
farmhand
and
presumed
dead,
but
Fiver
has
a
vision
and
follows
the
apparition
of
the
Black
Rabbit
of
Inle
to
his
injured
brother.
Kehaar
returns
and
while
removing
<a
href="/wiki/Buckshot?qsrc=3044"
title="Buckshot">buckshot</a>
pellets
from
Hazel's
leg,
reports
of
Efrafa,
a
large
warren
with
many
females.
Holly,
who
encountered
Efrafa,
begs
them
not
to
go
there,
describing
it
as
a
totalitarian
state,
run
by
vicious
and
heavily
territorial
rabbits.
Hazel
feels
they
have
no
choice
but
to
go
there.
Bigwig
infiltrates
the
colony
and
becomes
an
Owsla
officer
by
the
cruel
chief,
General
Woundwort.
Bigwig
recruits
several
potential
escapees
to
his
cause,
including
Hyzenthlay,
an
idealistic
doe
and
Blackavar,
a
scarred
attempted
escapee.
They
flee,
with
Woundwort
and
his
Owsla
in
pursuit.
Using
a
boat
to
float
down
the
river,
they
evade
capture.
That
night,
Keehar
leaves
for
his
homeland,
with
the
gratitude
of
the
warren.</p>








<p>Several
days
later,
Efrafan
trackers
discover
their
trail
and
follow
them
to
Watership
Down.
Hazel
offers
a
treaty
with
Woundwort,
who
dismisses
Hazel,
telling
him
to
have
Bigwig
and
the
deserters
surrender.
The
Watership
rabbits
barricade
their
warren
and
are
besieged
by
the
Efrafans.
Fiver
slips
into
a
<a
href="/wiki/Trance?qsrc=3044"
title="Trance">trance</a>,
in
which
he
envisions
a
dog
loose
in
the
woods.
His
moans
inspire
Hazel
to
free
the
dog
from
Nuthanger
and
lead
him
to
the
warren
to
intervene.
He
escapes
with
Blackberry,
Dandelion
and
Hyzenthlay.</p>








<p>Hazel
prays
to
Frith,
offering
his
life
for
the
those
in
the
warren,
a
bargain
Frith
acknowledges,
but
doesn't
accept,
as
the
outcome
is
ultimately
up
to
Hazel.
Hazel
frees
the
dog
while
his
companions
bait
it
into
following
them
to
Watership
Down.
When
the
Efrafans
break
through
the
warren's
defences,
Woundwort
leads
the
attack.
Blackavar
confronts
Woundwort,
but
is
overpowered
and
killed.
Bigwig
<a
href="/wiki/Ambush?qsrc=3044"
title="Ambush">ambushes</a>
Woundwort
and
they
fight
to
exhaustion.
The
dog
arrives
and
kills
the
Efrafan
soldiers.
Hearing
the
commotion,
Woundwort
abandons
Bigwig
and
confronts
the
dog.
No
trace
of
Woundwort
is
found,
leaving
his
fate
ambiguous.</p>








<p>Years
later,
the
warren
is
thriving.
An
elderly
Hazel
is
visited
by
the
Black
Rabbit,
who
invites
him
to
<a
href="/wiki/Death?qsrc=3044"
title="Death">join
his
Owsla</a>,
assuring
him
of
Watership
Down's
perpetual
safety.
Reassured,
Hazel
accepts
and
dies.
Hazel's
spirit
follows
the
Black
Rabbit
through
the
woodland
and
trees
towards
the
Sun,
which
metamorphoses
into
Frith
and
the
<a
href="/wiki/Afterlife?qsrc=3044"
title="Afterlife">afterlife</a>.</p>

<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Production">Production</span></h2>








<p>The
film
was
originally
to
be
directed
by
<a
href="/wiki/John_Hubley?qsrc=3044"
title="John
Hubley">John
Hubley</a>,
who
died
in
1977.
His
work
can
still
be
found
in
the
film,
most
notably
in
the
&quot;fable&quot;
scene.
He
was
replaced
by
the
film's
producer
Martin
Rosen,
his
directorial
debut.</p>








<p>After
the
genesis
story
rendered
in
a
narrated
simple
cartoon
fashion,
the
animation
style
changes
to
a
detailed,
naturalist
one,
with
concessions
to
render
the
animals
<a
href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic?qsrc=3044"
title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a>
only
to
suggest
they
have
human
voices
and
minds,
some
facial
expressions
for
emotion
and
paw
gestures.
The
animation
backgrounds
are
watercolours.
Only
one
of
the
predators,
the
farm
cat,
Tab,
is
given
a
few
lines,
the
rest
remaining
mute.</p>








<p>The
backgrounds
and
locations,
especially
Efrafa
and
the
nearby
railway,
are
based
to
the
diagrams
and
maps
in
Richard
Adams's
book.
Most
of
the
locations
in
this
movie
either
exist
or
were
based
on
real
spots
in
<a
href="/wiki/Hampshire?qsrc=3044"
title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>
and
surrounding
areas.</p>








<p>Although
the
film
was
fairly
faithful
to
the
novel,
several
changes
were
made
to
the
storyline,
mainly
to
decrease
overdetailed
complexity
and
improve
pace
and
flow
of
the
plot.
In
addition,
the
order
in
which
some
events
occur
is
re-arranged.
Unlike
many
animated
features,
the
film
faithfully
emulated
the
dark
and
violent
sophistication
of
the
book.
As
a
result,
many
reviewers
took
to
warning
parents
that
children
might
find
the
content
disturbing.
The
BBFC
rated
the
film
'U'
(suitable
for
all),
deciding
that
&quot;...Whilst
the
film
may
move
children
emotionally
during
the
films
duration,
it
could
not
seriously
trouble
them
once
the
spell
of
the
story
is
broken
and
a
'U'
certificate
was
therefore
quite
appropriate&quot;.
<sup
id="cite_ref-BBFC_Examiners_Report_15th_February_1978_1-0"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-BBFC_Examiners_Report_15th_February_1978-1">[2]</a></sup>
However
in
2012,
the
BBFC
admitted
that
it
&quot;has
received
complaints
about
the
suitability
of
Watership
Down
at
U
almost
every
year
since
its
classification&quot;<sup
id="cite_ref-2"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-2">[3]</a></sup></p>








<p>This
attitude
extended
to
when
the
animated
<a
href="/wiki/Watership_Down_(TV_series)?qsrc=3044"
title="Watership
Down
(TV
series)"><i>Watership
Down</i>
TV
series</a>
was
marketed
with
the
producers
making
an
effort
to
reassure
parents
that
the
violence
was
softened
and
that
the
main
characters
would
not
be
permanently
harmed
in
their
adventures.</p>








<p>Some
marketers
in
the
U.S.
were
also
worried
that
the
main
promotional
poster
(see
above)
appeared
too
dark
and
might
scare
some
children.
The
poster
is
actually
showing
Bigwig
in
a
snare
(his
distinctive
fur
is
clearly
visible),
and
the
image
on
the
poster
does
not
appear
in
the
film,
which
has
a
far
bloodier
depiction
of
the
scene.</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Music">Music</span></h3>








<p>The
musical
score
was
by
<a
href="/wiki/Angela_Morley?qsrc=3044"
title="Angela
Morley">Angela
Morley</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Malcolm_Williamson?qsrc=3044"
title="Malcolm
Williamson">Malcolm
Williamson</a>,
Morley
replacing
Williamson
after
the
composer
had
fallen
behind
and
only
composed
the
prelude
and
main
title
theme
in
sketch
form.<sup
id="cite_ref-3"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-3">[4]</a></sup>
A
list
of
the
musical
cues
for
the
film
can
be
found
on
the
composer's
website,
which
also
gives
information
about
the
different
composers
working
on
the
project.<sup
id="cite_ref-4"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-4">[5]</a></sup></p>








<p>The
soundtrack
included
<a
href="/wiki/Art_Garfunkel?qsrc=3044"
title="Art
Garfunkel">Art
Garfunkel</a>'s
British
No.
1
hit,
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Bright_Eyes_(Art_Garfunkel_song)?qsrc=3044"
title="Bright
Eyes
(Art
Garfunkel
song)">Bright
Eyes</a>&quot;,
which
was
written
by
the
British
singer
and
songwriter
<a
href="/wiki/Mike_Batt?qsrc=3044"
title="Mike
Batt">Mike
Batt</a>
(although
in
a
different
arrangement
from
the
one
released
as
a
single).
Batt
also
wrote
other
songs
for
the
film
which
were
not
used.
The
composer
recorded
three
songs
with
vocals
by
Garfunkel,
but
only
&quot;Bright
Eyes&quot;

<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Reception">Reception</span></h2>








<p>The
film
has
received
a
generally
positive
critical
reception,
with
an
81%
'Fresh'
rating
on
Rotten
Tomatoes,
and
a
rating
of
67%
from
select
critics.<sup
id="cite_ref-5"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-5">[6]</a></sup>
The
film
was
nominated
for
<a
href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation?qsrc=3044"
title="Hugo
Award
for
Best
Dramatic
Presentation">Hugo
Award
for
Best
Dramatic
Presentation</a>
in
1979.
In
2004,
the
magazine
<i><a
href="/wiki/Total_Film?qsrc=3044"
title="Total
Film">Total
Film</a></i>
named
<i>Watership
Down</i>
the
47th
greatest
British
film
of
all
time
and
it
was
also
ranked
15th
in
the
&quot;100
Greatest
Tearjerkers&quot;.</p>








<p>Investors
in
the
film
reportedly
received
a
return
of
5,000%
on
their
investment.<sup
id="cite_ref-icon_6-0"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-icon-6">[7]</a></sup></p>
