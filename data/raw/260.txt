<p>Robin
Gibb,
one-third
of
the
Bee
Gees,
died
Sunday
after
a
long
battle
with
cancer,
his
spokesperson
has
confirmed
via
a
statement.&nbsp;Gibb
was
62
years
old.</p>











<p>&quot;The
family
of
Robin
Gibb,
of
the
Bee
Gees,
announce
with
great
sadness
that
Robin
passed
away
today
following
his
long
battle
with
cancer
and
intestinal
surgery,&quot;
reads
the
statement.
&quot;The
family
have
asked
that
their
privacy
is
respected
at
this
very
difficult
time.&quot;</p>











<p>Two
years
ago,
Gibb
battled
colon
and
liver
cancer,
but
despite
making
what
he
called
a
&quot;spectacular
recovery,&quot;
a
secondary
tumor
recently
developed,
complicated
by
a
case
of
pneumonia
in
April.
The
singer
was
hospitalized
last
month
and
<a
href="../../../music/news/report-robin-gibb-in-a-coma-20120414">fell
into
a
coma</a>
at
one
point,
although
he
was
later
said
to
have
<a
href="../../../music/news/robin-gibb-wakes-from-coma-20120421">regained
consciousness</a>
and
communicated
with
family
members.&nbsp;</p>











<p>Gibb
was
born
in
the
Isle
of
Man
in
1949,
along
with
twin
brother
Maurice.
(Maurice
died
in
2003
of
complications
from
a
twisted
intestine;
eerily,
Robin
had
surgery
for
the
same
medical
issue
in
2010.)
Along
with
their
older
brother
Barry,
the
brothers
began
harmonizing
as
a
trio
in
Australia,
where
the
family
moved
in
1958.
Although
the
Bee
Gees
had
some
success
in
Australia
–
they
hosted
a
weekly
variety
show
there
–
they
didn't
truly
arrive
until
they
returned
to
England
and
signed
with
manager
Robert
Stigwood.
Robin's
quivering,
vulnerable
voice
was
featured
prominently
on
several
of
the
group's
earliest
and
most
Beatles-eque
hits,
including
&quot;New
York
Mining
Disaster
1941,&quot;
&quot;I
Started
a
Joke,&quot;
&quot;Massachusetts,&quot;
and
&quot;I've
Gotta
Get
a
Message
to
You.&quot;</p>











<p>Although
he
looked
and
sounded
like
the
meekest
Bee
Gee,
Robin
grew
into
the
family
rebel.
By
1969,
he
and
Barry
were
feuding
over
whose
song
should
be
singles,
and
Robin,
then
20,
was
declared
a
&quot;ward
of
the
state&quot;
by
their
father
when
his
drinking
and
partying
seemed
to
take
over
his
life.
&quot;It
happened
so
fast
that
we
lost
communication
between
us,&quot;
Gibb
later
recalled.
&quot;It
was
just
madness,
really.&quot;</p>











<p>But
it
was
also
Robin
who,
in
1971,
made
the
first
call
to
Barry
to
reunite
with
his
brothers.
Robin's
solo
career
had
stalled,
and
Barry
and
Maurice's
attempts
to
continue
the
Bee
Gees
as
a
duo
had
floundered
as
well.
&quot;If
we
hadn't
been
related,
we
would
probably
have
never
gotten
back
together,&quot;
Robin
said
at
the
time.
Robin's
voice
was
heard,
beautifully,
on
the
chorus
of
their
minor
1972
hit
&quot;Run
to
Me.&quot;&nbsp;</p>











<p>The
Bee
Gees'
massive
second
wind
arrived
with
their
proto
disco
hit,
&quot;Jive
Talkin',&quot;
in
1975;
two
years
later,
their
contributions
to
<em>Saturday
Night
Fever</em>
made
them
bigger
stars
than
ever.
Most
of
the
hits
from
that
era
featured
Barry's
falsetto
voice,
but
the
brothers'
vocal
blend
remained
an
indelible
part
of
their
sound.</p>











<p>The
group
entered
another
fallow
period
during
the
early
Eighties,
although
during
this
time,
Robin
produced
a
semi-hit
album
by
Jimmy
Ruffin,
brother
of
the
Temptations'
David
Ruffin.
The
last
Bee
Gees
album,
<em>This
Is
Where
I
Came
In</em>,
was
released
in
2001.
Two
years
later,
Maurice
died,
and
with
his
passing
the
Bee
Gees
ended.
(Their
other,
younger
brother
Andy
died
in
1988.)</p>











<p>Robin
and
Barry
reunited
periodically
–
in
2010,
they
made
an
appearance
on
<em>American
Idol</em>
and
inducted
ABBA
into
the
Rock
and
Roll
Hall
of
Fame
–
and
talked
about
a
duo
tour,
but
nothing
materialized.
Robin,
though,
kept
his
hand
in
music.
With
his
son
Robin-John,
he
wrote
an
ambitious
piece,
<em>The
Titanic
Requiem</em>,
a
mix
of
orchestral
and
vocal
pieces
telling
the
story
of
the
doomed
liner
on
the
100<sup>th</sup>
anniversary
of
its
sinking.
&quot;It's
a
serious
subject
and
it's
not
a
rock
opera,&quot;
Gibb
said
before
its
debut.
&quot;There
are
no
backbeats.
This
could
have
been
written
300
years
ago.&quot;</p>











<p>Featuring
the
Royal
Philharmonic
Orchestra,
the
work
had
its
world
premiere
in
London
on
April
10th.
But
in
a
sign
that
Gibb's
health
had
taken
a
turn
for
the
worse,
he
wasn't
able
to
attend.&nbsp;</p>

<h4>“Those
Damn
Blue
Collar
Tweekers”</h4>












<span>Primus&nbsp;|&nbsp;1991</span>












<p>The
songs
that
comprise
Primus'
1991
album,
<em>Sailing
the
Seas
of
Cheese</em>,
are
loaded
with
topics
that
remain
uncommon
in
the
rock
format
--
a
warning
against
&quot;a
hammerhead
shark,
nose
hairs
and
flatus&quot;
(&quot;Here
Come
the
Bastards&quot;),
a
studly
feline
(&quot;Tommy
the
Cat&quot;)
and
eating
taco-flavored
Doritos
on
a
boat
(&quot;Fish
On&quot;).
And
&quot;Those
Damn
Blue
Collar
Tweekers&quot;
can
also
be
filed
under
this
category.
&quot;A
tweaker
is
someone
who
is
strung
out
on
methyl
amphetamines,
otherwise
known
as
crank,&quot;
Les
Claypool
told
<em>Rolling
Stone</em>.
&quot;There's
a
reference
in
there
to
a
guy
who
hung
Sheetrock,
and
that's
how
he
got
through
the
day.
He'd
snort
up
speed
to
keep
up
with
the
younger
guys.&quot;
When
&quot;Tweekers&quot;
was
performed
at
Woodstock
'94,
Claypool
inserted
in
a
bit
of
&quot;The
Star
Spangled
Banner&quot;
on
his
bass,
mid-song,
as
a
sideways
Hendrix
tribute.</p>
